#+TITLE: Data UI: Describe Your App, Click, and It's Live
#+AUTHOR: Donnie
#+DATE: 2026

* You Need an App. You Don't Need a Developer.

You have an idea. A task tracker for your team. A shared inventory
for your business. A directory of members for your club. You know
exactly what it should do — a list of items, a few fields, maybe
categories, maybe the ability to upload photos.

So you start looking into how to build it, and here's what you find:
you need a database, a backend, an API, a frontend, user accounts,
login, permissions, a server, a domain name, a security certificate,
and someone who understands all of it. The quotes come back at
$15,000. Or $40,000. Or "we can get you an MVP in eight weeks."

Data UI does all of it. You describe what you want in a short file,
run one command, and your application is live on the internet — with
user accounts, with permissions, with a clean interface — in minutes.

No developer required.

* What Do You Mean, "Describe It"?

Think of it like a recipe. A recipe doesn't build the cake — it
/tells you/ what goes into the cake. Data UI works the same way. You
write a short description of your data:

- What things does your app track? (Tasks, products, members,
  recipes, properties — whatever you need)
- What fields does each thing have? (A name, a description, a
  checkbox for "done," a category, a photo)
- How do they relate? (A task can have tags, a product belongs to a
  category)

That description is called a /model/. It's a single file, usually
shorter than this page. From that file, Data UI builds everything:
the database, the user interface, the login system, the permissions,
the web server, the security — all of it.

* But I Don't Know How to Write Code

Here's the part that surprises people: **you don't have to start from
scratch.**

Data UI has something called the **Model Bank** — a library of
ready-made models that other people have created and shared. It works
like an app store, but instead of downloading finished apps, you
browse models: short descriptions of applications that are ready to
deploy.

**Here's what the process looks like:**

1. *Browse the Model Bank.* You see a to-do list app, an inventory
   tracker, a member directory, a recipe collection, a project
   board. Each one is a working application, described in a small
   file.

2. *Find one that's close to what you need.* Maybe you want a task
   tracker, but you also want a "priority" field. Or you want the
   member directory, but you need to track membership expiration
   dates.

3. *Copy it.* One click. The model is now yours.

4. *Make your changes.* This is the part that sounds scary but
   isn't. The model is just a list of fields and their types. Adding
   a field looks like this:

   #+begin_src lisp
   :priority
   (:type :text
     :ui (:label "Priority" :input-type :select))
   #+end_src

   That's it. You added a dropdown called "Priority" to your task
   tracker. You didn't write a database migration. You didn't touch
   the frontend. You didn't configure anything. You added five lines
   to a file.

5. *Deploy.* One command. Your customized app is live on the
   internet, with its own address, behind encryption, ready to use.

**From idea to live app, the whole thing can take ten minutes.** And
most of that time is you deciding what fields you want.

* What If I Get Stuck?

The model format is designed to be readable. It's not code in the
traditional sense — there are no functions, no logic, no control
flow. It's a structured list of /what your data looks like/, written
in a format that's mostly just keywords and values.

If you can fill out a form, you can edit a model. And because models
are small (often under a page), you can see the whole thing at once
and understand what each part does.

Post-MVP, Data UI will also accept models in YAML and JSON — formats
that are even more approachable for non-technical users — and will
offer an AI assistant that can generate or modify models from plain
English descriptions. "I need an app to track book loans for my
library, with borrower names, book titles, and due dates" will
produce a model you can review, tweak, and deploy.

* What You Actually Get

Every Data UI app comes with these features, automatically, with no
extra effort on your part:

** User Accounts and Login

People sign up, log in, and manage their own accounts. Secure
passwords. Session management. Token-based authentication. You don't
configure any of it — it's just there.

** Permissions and Roles

Not everyone should see everything. Data UI handles this from the
start. You can have admins who can do anything, regular users who can
only see their own data, or any role structure you need. And you
manage roles through the same simple interface as everything else —
assigning a role to a user is as easy as checking a box.

** A Clean, Working Interface

Lists of your data. Forms to add and edit items. Checkboxes for
yes/no fields. Dropdowns for categories. Image upload with thumbnail
previews. Delete buttons (only shown to people who have permission to
delete). It's not flashy, but it's clean, functional, and works on
any device with a browser.

** Your Own Web Address

When you deploy, your app gets its own URL. It's served over HTTPS
(encrypted, secure). You can share the link with anyone and they can
use your app immediately.

** A Real Database

Your data is stored in PostgreSQL — an industrial-strength database
used by major companies worldwide. It's not a spreadsheet or a flat
file. It's the real thing, with proper constraints, data integrity,
and backup capability.

* How Is This Possible?

The secret is that most applications are mostly the same. A task
tracker, an inventory system, a member directory — they all have the
same bones: lists of things, fields on those things, relationships
between things, user accounts, and permissions. What changes is the
specifics: /which/ things, /which/ fields, /which/ relationships.

Data UI captures the specifics in a small model and handles all the
common machinery itself. It's a /compiler/ — the same kind of tool
that turns source code into running software, but instead of
translating programming instructions, it translates your data
description into a complete, working application.

This is why the model stays small: you only describe what's /unique/
about your app. Everything else — the database setup, the API, the
security, the deployment — is the compiler's job.

* How Is This Different from [Other Tool]?

You've maybe seen website builders, no-code platforms, or low-code
tools. Here's what makes Data UI different:

- *Website builders* (Wix, Squarespace) make beautiful /pages/. They
  don't make data-driven applications with user accounts, permissions,
  and complex relationships.

- *No-code platforms* (Bubble, Glide) can build apps, but you build
  them by dragging boxes on a canvas and clicking through menus. The
  result is locked inside their platform. If they change their
  pricing or shut down, your app is gone. And the logic is spread
  across dozens of UI panels that are hard to review or share.

- *Data UI* puts your entire application in a single, small,
  human-readable file. You can email it. You can put it in version
  control. You can hand it to someone else and they can understand
  it. And because it's open source, you can run it anywhere — no
  vendor lock-in.

The model /is/ the application. There's nothing else to maintain.

* A Concrete Example

Let's say you run a small library and want to track book loans.

You browse the Model Bank and find a "Member Directory" model. It
tracks people with names, emails, and phone numbers. Close, but you
need more.

You copy the model and add:

- A =books= section with title, author, and ISBN fields
- A =loans= section that connects a member to a book, with a due-date
  field
- A =returned= checkbox on each loan

That's maybe twenty lines of additions to a file you already
understand. You deploy, and now you have:

- A working web app at your own URL
- Members who can log in
- A list of books you can add to and edit
- A loan tracker that shows who has what and when it's due
- Admin controls so only you can mark loans as returned

Total time: maybe thirty minutes, and most of that was you thinking
about what fields you wanted.
